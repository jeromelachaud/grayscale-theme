Effects of Auditory Feedback on Menu Selection in Hand-Gesture Interfaces

Josh Mills

2016-02-25

Focus of this study

One of the reasons why people are hesitant to accept hand-gesture interfaces as an alternative input method is their lack of secondary feedback. Therefore, the focus of this study is on secondary feedback.

The proposal

we propose using auditory feedback to complement visual feedback and make menu selection in gesture-based interfaces more efficient

Gesture Interface Example

This gesture interface uses two simple hand gestures, as in Figure 1b. Users can browse the menu with circular motions of the hand; the touching gesture of moving the hand forward selects the menu item

Article's Aim

This article aims to study how users can access the circular menu with hand gestures and how much auditory feedback can affect the usability of menu selection in hand-gesture interfaces

Conventional Auditory Feedback

This gesture interface uses two simple hand gestures, as in Figure 1b. Users can browse the menu with circular motions of the hand; the touching gesture of moving the hand forward selects the menu item

Issue with transitional auditory feedback for gestural interface

Weaknesses in transitional auditory feedback lie in the fact that it provides limited information about gesture position in the circular menu

Solution

Preemptive Auditory Feedback

Having more robust infor- mation about the transition between menu items will aid users in making more efficient and accurate menu selections in the gesture interface

Another Solution

Continuous Auditory Feedback

An alternative to generating auditory feedback at specific locations in the circular menu is to provide the user with constant auditory feedback. Using Shepard’s tone creates an auditory illusion of infinitely descending or ascending pitches to the listener, which semantically fits in with circular menus

When it comes to the touching gesture, finding the location of the touch surface is a difficult task for the user

we propose using a continuous depth auditory feedback with frequency and amplitude that increases when the hand approaches the touch surface

Study Design

the developed application used a circular menu as the method for participants to make selections

Results

The difference in selection times between the transitional auditory feedback and visual-only feedback conditions was not significant

The preemptive auditory feedback did not yield significant difference in selection time when compared with visual-only and transitional auditory feedback conditions, indicating that participants selected slightly faster when using continuous auditory feedback and depth auditory feedback

Accuracy

participants selected more accurately when using preemptive auditory feedback and depth auditory feedback

Overall, depth auditory feedback gave the best performance, achieving the highest accu- racy and the quickest selection time

It was the only feedback that yielded significantly better performance than both visual-only feedback and conventional or tran- sitional auditory feedback

The mean selection time of the transitional auditory feedback condition was 2.76 seconds, 4.4 percent faster than the visual-only feedback

This means that the conventional auditory feedback that gives simple sounds only at the boundaries of the circular menu helps achieve quicker selection times

Continuous Auditory Feedback

When using continuous auditory feedback, users were informed about the direction and speed of the browsing gesture movement through sounds, which helped them select the designated menu faster

Depth Auditory Feedback

Using the depth audi- tory feedback, users become aware of the touch surface, which enabled them to browse more confidently because they knew that they weren’t going to select menu items by accident.

The mean selection accuracy of the visual-only feedback condition was 90 per- cent; for the transitional auditory feedback con- dition, it was 89.5 percent, which was close to the visual-only feedback condition
