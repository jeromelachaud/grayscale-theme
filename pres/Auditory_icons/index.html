Auditory Icons

This paper looks at the affect of using auditory icons as a warning system in an environment where warnings are prevalent and plentiful, such as a control room or a hospital

“sounds that provide information about an event that represents desired data“ -Gaver

Gaver describes auditory icons as sounds that provide information about an event that represents desired data.

caricatures of naturally occurring sounds

Or as caricatures of naturally occurring sounds.

Aim of this study

The aim of this study was to determine the number of auditory icons that can be learned, in a control room context.

Procedure

The test subjects were separated into three groups, where the first group was tested on 10 icons, the second on 20 and the third on 30.

Training

The subjects experienced a training session directly before the testing session where they were able to here the icons while being shown what they referenced.

What I found interesting..

One thing that I found interesting was the fact that none of their test subjects had hearing issues. I wonder how hearing impairments would come into play and effect auditory icons.

Testing

The subjects were tested directly after the training session, and during testing an icon was played to them, and they then had to match that icon sound to its referent.

Results

The results showed great success for the auditory icon warnings as 100% of the first group were able to correctly match the icons, the majority of the second group was able to match 100% correctly as well, and 93% of the third group was able to accurately match the icons

What does this mean/show?

I think that the important thing to get from the results is the fact that doubling and even tripling the amount of icons that the subjects had to keep track of had little to no effect on the subjects correctly matching the icons.

10 icon limit in aircraft (Patterson and Mayfield)

This debunks the previously noted hypothesis that 10 or more auditory warnings in an aircraft context/environment is excessive. The subjects in this study were able to score almost perfectly with 30 icons even though they had little previous exposure to them.

So why are auditory warnings so easy to learn and replicate?

According to Fagerlönn and Alm

who found that the use of sounds which are meaningful in the context in which they are applied can reduce the number of required cognitive resources compared with the use of arbitrarily mapped sounds

Direct Relationship

The aim of the study was to use sounds with a direct relationship to the object/event that they were supposed to represent, because direct relations create rapid and immediate recognition of the target

Indirect Relationship

indirect associations are also an excellent alternative when direct associations are not feasible.

Further improvements

While I think that using auditory icons as warnings is a great application, I feel that there are still some ways to improve upon the practice. The paper mentions, and I agree, that involving the users into the design process will allow for even stronger relationships to be noticed and used when coming up with auditory icons. The users themselves are experts on their working environments and therefore may be very comfortable or aware of certain distinguishable sounds already. This would only make the learning process easier and faster.

Issues

One issue that was noted was the incorrect pairings that happened multiple times.
It turns out that this is attributed to the fact that the sounds were similar, for example both 15 and 22 were similar to a snap/slam sound. This can be solved though by simply involving the user in order to discover similar sounds.

Real-world Application

The study was conducted in a control room environment, but I can see this being very practical for many other environments as well including airplane cockpits, cars, hospitals, etc.
