Modeling visual sampling on in-car displays: The challenge of predicting safety-critical lapses of control

Josh Mills

2016-01-28

Research Area

This paper presents a novel mathematical model for visual search and selection time in linear menus.

Strategies used:

serial

directed

Two search strategies were assumed, serial and directed in relation to a pointing sub-task

Purpose

• The model has the potential to improve menu designs by helping designers identify more effective solutions without conducting empirical studies • i.e. to design better menus • The goal is to advance the scope and validity of mathematical models of linear menus

Analysis

Absent Search Effect:

Absent target effect: It can be seen that participants were initially very slow at making these absent target decisions during the first block of trials and by the last block, participants were very quick at indicating when there was an absent target.

Practice Effect: (Table 1)

Practice effect: Table 1 shows that there was a consistent effect of block, reflecting that users became faster at locating the target with repeated practice at searching the same menu

Target Position Effect Effect: (Figure 2)

Target position effect: There was a robust effect of Target position on search time (Figure 2)

Menu Length Effect:

Menu length effect: Selection times were longer for targets in a given position when the menu contained more items.

Menu Organization Effect:

Menu organization effect: Participants were faster at selecting targets when the menu had either an alphabetic or semantic organization compared to when it had an unordered organization

Last Item Effect:

Last item effect: It is the opposite of what would be expected by Fitts’ law, that items positioned last are faster to select

Applying the Results

This paper advances the understanding of menu performance by providing for pointing behavior and visual search. It achieves this by combining assumptions about search and pointing strategies that have been identified by previous literature but never modeled in a single model

Explanation

• Explanation: The model increases our ability to cover important design factors by a single model, the model covers the effects of menu organization, absent item, and target position, as well as the previously modeled factors of menu length and experience. Model also proposes counter-intuitive “last item effect”
